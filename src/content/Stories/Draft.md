# Chapter 1

Blood. It dripped in slow, deliberate pools staining the cold marble under me. Cold fluorescent lights flickered erratically, casting long jagged shadows across walls lined with equipment that no longer hummed with purpose. Broken glass shimmered on the floor, each shard catching the pale light like frozen tears. The air reeked with the scent of iron and copper, a sharp odor that reminded me that this wasn't a nightmare I could wake up from.

I was shocked. I stood at the doorway, my fingers clutching the frame of the door so hard that the only thing tethering me to reality was the pain. The buzz of machinery, once rhythmic and soothing, now turned into silence, leaving only the deafening static of my heartbeat.

There, in the centre of the room, was my father.

Dr. Atlas Ambrose. The man who had unlocked the secrets of the quantum realm, who could explain the intricacies of the universe in a single breath, now crumpled like a ragdoll. His once pristine white lab coat, which had always smelled of chalk and coffee, was soaked in crimson. The blood was spreading endlessly, creeping onto the floor in thing spidery veins that threatened to reach me.

"Some moments don't just change us, they reshape us making a path forward that we never saw coming. Forcing us to walk on it whether we are ready or not." The words that came out of his mouth not more than a week ago sounded like a prophecy to my ears, made for this very moment.

I couldn't move.

The room seemed huge, spreading into the void. It swallowed me whole, allowing me to view only my father's lifeless body and the man standing over him. The figure was an enigma of darkness. His silhouette was sharp and menacing against the mayhem. He did not belong here. His dark coat and gloved hands, pure among the devastations, seemed to present him as more ghostly than human.

“Boy,” he said, his voice smooth and detached, like the scrape of a knife against stone.

I flinched.

“You shouldn’t have seen this.”

I couldn't breathe, nor could I move, nor speak. My chest tightened with each passing second, the world around me collapsing into a cold, suffocating void. I felt so powerless—cripplingly powerless—in the face of losing what I cherished the most. The man stood motionless for a moment, his silhouette cutting an imposing figure against the bloodstained floor. Slowly, with a deliberate and practiced motion, he tucked the gun into a holster beneath his coat. His fingers hesitated for the briefest moment, just enough for a flicker of unease to cross my mind, before he let the weapon settle into place. He turned without a word. His boots clicked softly against the marble, each step deliberate, echoing down the long, empty hall. My legs screamed to move, to chase after him, but I was frozen in place, trapped by a storm of grief and disbelief. With great effort, I forced the words out, my voice cracking under their weight.

"I... I will find you."

He paused mid-step, the air between us growing impossibly heavy. His head tilted slightly, just enough to suggest he’d heard me. For a moment, the silence felt like it might swallow us both whole. Then he spoke.

"Maybe you will," he said, his voice smooth and detached, yet with an undercurrent of something I couldn’t place. "But not today. And definitely not as you are now." His voice was sharp, clean, like a razor honed to perfection. Yet there was something beneath it all—something unseen. It was not pity or arrogance, but something more quiet, more indistinct. "There is much more you must discover," he said, his voice low, almost reflective. "This world is far crueler and more accursed than you could ever have imagined."

He didn’t look back as he spoke, and before I could say anything more, he disappeared into the shadows, leaving only the echo of his footsteps behind.

For a moment, I didn't move. The silence pressed down on me, thick and suffocating. And then suddenly, as though a switched had been flipped, reality came to me.

"Dad!"

I fell to my knees, hitting the blood stained floor. My hands shivered as I pressed them against his chest, trying to stop the stream of blood. But it was futile - there was too much.

"Please," I whispered. "Please wake up. You can't... you can't leave me."

His face was pale, the deep lines of worry he always carried were now a lifeless form. The sharp angles of his features, which his smile often softened, froze into a mask of silence.

Something felt wrong with the air; it was like the lab itself was mourning. The walls, which once used to be pure white, were smeared with burn and scorch marks. The screens of his beloved monitors flickered with static, their broken circuits hissing faintly. I glanced at the desk where he used to work, the surface cluttered with notes and diagrams. Pages were scattered across the floor, their neat equations now torn and smudged. The small trinkets he kept—a worn photograph of my mother, a silver compass he used to fiddle with when lost in thought—lay untouched amidst the chaos.

I reached for the photograph, my fingers brushing against its edges. It was cracked, the image distorted, but I could still make out the faint smile on my mother’s face. The tears came then, hot and uncontrollable. They fell onto the photo, smearing the glass as I clutched it to my chest.

“You promised,” I whispered. My voice was hollow, breaking under the weight of the words. “You promised you’d never leave me.”

But promises didn’t mean anything. Not anymore.

I felt the blood raising into my eyes before everything went dark.

The sound of approaching footsteps snapped me out of my trance.

“Search the area!” a voice barked, sharp and commanding. “Find the boy.”

The lab was no longer safe. My last glance I made at my father, where my heart went and shattered itself once more. And then with one ragged breath, stood myself up straight.

And ran.

The city was a blur of rain and light.

I remember running — though “running” feels too simple a word for what it was. It was flight. Desperate, blind, and directionless. My feet struck the cracked pavement in rhythm with my heartbeat, each step carrying me further from the lab, yet never far enough from the image of my father’s body.

The streets were alive with indifference. Neon signs flickered above shuttered storefronts, their glow bleeding into the puddles at my feet. Somewhere, a siren wailed — distant, distorted — and for a moment, I thought it was his voice calling me back. I turned once, half expecting to see the shadowed figure behind me, but there was only the empty stretch of road, slick with rain and memory.

I kept moving.

Past the glass towers where people lived their lives behind curtains of light, past alleys that swallowed sound, past the edge of the city where the smell of metal and oil faded into damp earth and coal smoke. My breath tore at my throat. My mind screamed for me to stop, but something deeper — something born of grief and terror — dragged me onward.

By the time I reached the outskirts, dawn was nothing more than a pale bruise on the horizon. My legs gave out, and I fell to my knees beside an iron gate, its paint peeling in long, rusted strips. A sign hung crooked above it, words nearly erased by time:

**St. Elaira’s Home for Lost Children.**

Lost.

The word lingered, cruelly fitting. I pushed the gate open and stepped inside.

# Chapter 2

The orphanage was nothing like I had imagined - not that I'd had imagined anything.

The air was thick with dampness, heavy with the scent of mildew and coal smoke drifting from a nearby factory. The building stood before me like a relic of grief — its once-white walls streaked with moss, ivy climbing the stone like skeletal fingers reaching for the sky. The wooden doors loomed ahead, carved with cracks that looked like veins, and as I reached for the handle, the wind sighed through the iron gate behind me.

When the door opened, the world outside seemed to fade.

The orphanage sat there like a relic - its once white facade streaked with moss. Ivy climbed up the walls twisting like skeletal fingers towards the cracked windows. The gates swung open ominously as I pushed them, stepping onto a gravel path that led to the oak doors.

The air was thick with dampness, with a scent of mildew mingling in the faint tang of coal smoke from a factory nearby. It was as if the world wanted to remind that hope was fleeting.

A woman with a severe face and grey hair greeted me at the door. Her expression didn't soften when she saw me, if anything the lines around her mouth deepened into something almost cruel.

"Do you have a name? Or are you just another stray?"

I wanted to say something but my throat felt raw, my words were caught up somewhere between despair and defiance.

"Another stray it is." She sighed, waving me inside without a word, and I entered what would be my new prison.

The inside was no less bleak than the exterior. The walls were painted a dull, institutional green, and the floors were scuffed and worn, creaking with every step. A single lightbulb swung erratically on the ceiling, casting long and erratic shadows that seemed to dance on the walls.

The other children stared at me as the matron walked me down the hall. Their eyes were sunken, cheeks hollow, and their clothes hung from their frames like loose rags. Whispers followed me, but I had learnt to ignore them, focusing instead on the rhythmic squeak of the matron's shoes.

My room was on the end of the hall, a small room which was barely enough for the narrow cot and rusted dresser it contained.

"Welcome to your new home." The matron said with a fleeting smile as she shut the door behind her.

For the first time I fled the lab I truly felt alone. I lay cold, on my lumpy mattress, my body too weak to move but my mind racing in a torment of memory and regret. The room was silent except for the creak of the old building setting into the night. Yet, I couldn't stop hearing it - the gun shot, the shouts and the echos of my dad's voice amidst the chaos. The weight pressed onto my chest and made it hard for me to breathe.

The blood. It was everywhere. The sickly metallic tang clung to my hands no matter how much I scrubbed my hands. I could still see it staining my nails. I could still feel the warmth of it soaking into my clothes. It wasn't just father's blood - it was my failure. I had just stood there, powerless unable to do anything as my life crumbled before me.

The endless loop of my fathers eyes, wide with shock and then dimming with acceptance played behind my own. I tried shutting my eyes harder trying to push the memory away but it only burned brighter. How could a man so invincible and of so much brilliance be reduced to this?

Tears started forming in the corner of my eyes but I forced them back, clenching my jaw tight. The matrons dismissive tone still rang through my head, her words branding me as just another stray to be tolerated, to be fed. No one here would understand. No one here would care. I felt so utterly alone. My warm and safe lifestyle had long gone. Snatched by forces I don't understand yet. For the first time, I felt small. Really small. Like a peck of dust in a universe that didn't care if i had lived or died. All the lessons that dad taught me, all the stories he had told me, all of them felt hollow now, fairy tales for kids who didn't know better. The cosmos I had once seen as beautiful was now just a vast and cruel place where gods played with the lives of mortals.

I curled in on myself and clutched the scratchy blanket like it was the only thing shielding me from the weight of everything. My breath was shallow, my throat shallow from choking back my sobs.

At that moment I had made a promise; I wouldn't let this end. I couldn't. I couldn't let my father's death go in vain. I would get to the bottom of this and find answers. And after that I would start a new life.

But for now all I could do was close my eyes and hope I could open them tomorrow.
